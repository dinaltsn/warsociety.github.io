Preparation — 4 months to session I

The Reading
Curriculum.

Each book builds directly on the last. By session one you have four intellectual lenses that work together in every game: geography, war as political logic, scenario construction, actor incentives, and system consequences.

The reason war games work intellectually is that they force you to think in constraints, not preferences. A country does not invade because it is evil — it invades because it has a 300 km indefensible plain to its west, no warm-water port, and an election in 6 months. Once you think that way, every scenario becomes tractable.

War Society — Core Principle
Month 1

The Foundation

Why geography is destiny — and why war is politics by another name.

Book 01

Prisoners of Geography

Tim Marshall

What to read

All chapters — re-read with annotation

The founding lens of the club. Every scenario you build starts with a map, and this book teaches you what to look for on it. Re-read with annotation: what resource, what border, what chokepoint.

Core concept

States do not choose their behaviour — their geography does it for them. A country with no warm-water port will always be desperate for one. Russia's obsession with buffer states, China's non-negotiable position on the South China Sea, the Middle East's arbitrary colonial borders — geography explains all of it.

Book 02

On War

Carl von Clausewitz — Book I only

What to read

Book I only — approx. 80 pages (Howard & Paret translation)

The philosophical backbone of the club. Justifies the entire Act III economic autopsy — if war is politics, then every military decision is also an economic decision.

Three concepts to extract

War is the continuation of politics by other means. Friction — plans never survive contact with reality. The fog of war — actors make decisions with incomplete information. Your sessions must replicate all three.

Month 2

Scenario Building

Structured uncertainty — how to construct a scenario that does not collapse into opinion.

Book 03

The Art of the Long View

Peter Schwartz

What to read

Chapters 1–6 — approx. 120 pages

The Act I construction method. After reading this, you can build a scenario card in 30 minutes. You will know how to set the stakes without predetermining the outcome.

The key framework

Two axes — Predetermined (things that will happen regardless: geography, demographics, debt levels) and Uncertain (things that could go either way: elections, a leader's health, weather). Plot a 2×2 grid of scenarios from the intersections. Each quadrant is a plausible future. None is the correct one.

Month 3

Actor Logic

Why states do what they do — incentives, deterrence, credible commitment.

Book 04

The Strategy of Conflict

Thomas Schelling — Nobel Prize in Economics, 2005

What to read

Chapters 1, 2, 3 — approx. 80 pages. Read slowly.

The engine of Act II. Dense but precise. Each actor card in your sessions has a public commitment section — something the actor has said publicly that they cannot walk back. Schelling explains precisely why those commitments trap actors into moves they might not otherwise choose.

Four concepts to master

A threat is only useful if it is credible — an incredible threat reveals weakness. Credible commitment: why backing down from a public commitment is often more costly than the original conflict. Deterrence by denial vs. deterrence by punishment. Schelling's focal points — why actors sometimes coordinate without communicating.

Month 4

Consequences

Systems and economic fallout — seeing feedback loops in geopolitical decisions.

Book 05

Thinking in Systems

Donella Meadows

What to read

All — 220 pages. One of the most efficient books you will read.

The language and method for the Act III economic autopsy. After each session, you draw the causal feedback loop on the whiteboard. Meadows teaches you to trace that chain — not intuitively but structurally. This is what separates War Society from a debate club.

What to understand

Feedback loops: every action creates a response that feeds back into the original system. Stocks and flows: how resources accumulate and deplete. System archetypes: 'escalation', 'tragedy of the commons', 'shifting the burden' — these are recurring patterns in every geopolitical scenario you will ever run.

Reading Notes Method

After finishing each book, write one page of notes — not a summary, but three ideas you could explain clearly to someone else. By month four you have four pages. That is your personal facilitator's handbook for every session that follows.

Optional — add after month four

For members who want to go deeper before session two.

Thinking, Fast and Slow

Daniel Kahneman

Why actors make irrational decisions under pressure. Adds a behavioural layer to Schelling — the missing piece when structural logic fails to explain a move.

The Sleepwalkers

Christopher Clark

How WWI actually started. The best case study in unintended escalation ever written. Essential reading for understanding friction card logic.

Prisoners of Geography: 10 Maps

Tim Marshall (follow-up)

Maps focused on Africa, the Arctic, and climate. Future session material — the geography of the next generation of crises.
