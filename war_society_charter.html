Society Charter

The rules
of the room

This is not a debate club. It is not a lecture. It is an analytical exercise — and the quality of the analysis depends entirely on how members speak to one another.

The one white page — conduct, clarity, and the presumption of good faith
I How we speak
  • Argue the position, not the person
    Challenge what someone said, not who they are. If you disagree with a move, name the structural reason it fails — do not name the player who made it. The actor at the table is not the country they are playing.
  • Speak in constraints, not preferences
    The word "should" signals an opinion. Replace it, always, with "can, given the constraints." This is not a stylistic rule — it is the entire intellectual method of the club. Members who cannot make this shift are not yet thinking analytically.
  • One voice at a time. Always.
    You will be heard. Interrupting does not make your point stronger — it signals that you stopped listening before the other person finished. Wait. The room rewards patience.
  • Short sentences carry more weight than long ones
    A precise observation in two sentences is worth more than a rambling paragraph. Practise saying what you mean in fewer words. The members who speak most clearly, not most often, shape the session.
II How we disagree
  • Disagreement is the point
    This club exists because geopolitics is genuinely contested. Two members can analyse the same scenario and reach opposite conclusions — both can be structurally valid. Disagreement is not a failure of the session. Agreement reached too quickly is.
  • State your evidence, then your conclusion
    Do not lead with a claim and hunt for justification afterwards. Lead with the structural fact — the geography, the debt level, the public commitment — and let the conclusion follow naturally. If the conclusion cannot survive that order, it is not a conclusion.
  • Concede when the argument is lost
    If someone makes a point that invalidates yours, say so. "That changes my read" is one of the most analytically honest sentences in the room. Members who cannot concede lose credibility faster than members who are occasionally wrong and admit it.
III How we prepare
  • Come having read the material
    The assigned reading is not optional context — it is the shared vocabulary of the session. Members who have not read it consume the time of those who have. If you did not read it, say so at the start. Do not discover this mid-session.
  • Bring your ignorance, not your certainty
    The most useful question in a session is one you genuinely do not know the answer to. Performing expertise degrades the room. A sincere "I don't know — what would the map tell us?" is worth more than a confident wrong answer.
  • Your actor card is a constraint, not a character
    Playing Russia does not mean you endorse Russia. You are modelling structural logic, not performing ideological allegiance. The quality of your analysis depends on your willingness to argue for a position you personally reject.
IV How we treat each other
  • Standing is earned at the table, not assumed
    A member's worth in a session is measured by the quality of their structural reasoning — not their background, nationality, politics, or how long they have been a member. The newest member who makes the most precise observation wins the room.
  • No mockery of a move, ever
    If a player makes a structurally poor decision, engage with it analytically. There is almost always a structural logic behind even terrible decisions — that is precisely what makes real geopolitics so dangerous. Mocking a move closes the most interesting question in the room.
  • The room is confidential
    What is said in session stays in session. Members take the structural lessons with them — not the specific words, attributions, or positions of individual players. This is what makes it possible to argue a position you do not actually hold.
V What is not permitted

These behaviours end the session or end the membership

  • Moral characterisation of actors in place of structural analysis. "Russia is evil" is not a move. It is a reason to stop the session.
  • Personal attacks on other members — for their position, their background, or the actor they were assigned.
  • Performative certainty about genuinely uncertain things. Claiming to know what a government will do is not analysis. It is noise.
  • Bringing personal politics to the table as evidence. Your allegiances are not a structural argument. They do not belong here.
  • Repeating a point that has already been refuted without addressing the refutation. Say something new or yield the floor.

VI The standard we hold ourselves to
  • Leave less certain than you arrived
    Certainty that survives a session was not tested. The mark of a good session is not that everyone agreed on an outcome — it is that everyone left with a sharper sense of what they do not yet understand. That is the only intellectual progress worth making.
  • The best analysis costs you something
    If your argument never required you to defend a position you find uncomfortable, you have not been challenged. Play the actor you were given. Make the case the evidence demands. The discomfort is not a side effect of good analysis — it is the proof that it happened.
